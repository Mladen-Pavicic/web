Comparative analysis of how a

Scientific Board for Physics

handled referees' reports on the project proposal

Mladen Pavicic,
Quantum Computation: Parallelism and Visualization



At the end of 2006 the Ministry of Science, Education, and Sport (the Ministry for short) following a proposal of its Scientific Board for Physics approved budgets for 72 project proposals in physics.

The only project in the field of quantum computation in Croatia, i.e., Mladen Pavicic, Quantum Computation: Parallelism and Visualization is not among them although two referees (one from Croatia and one from abroad) gave the proposal the average of 4.3 scores out of 5 possible ones and advised the Scientific Board to propose the Ministry to budget the project. As it is well-known, the field is one the most propulsive and the fastest growing (multidisciplinary) research fields in the World (each year more and more billions of dollars are put into it). To evaluate how important the field is taken to be by the physics community in the World it suffices to see how big is the section Quantum Information in the Physical Review A.

The Scientific Board rejected the positive evaluation of the referees and their advice to accept the project without giving a reason or argument for doing so and without laying down any methodology that would approve such a decision.

Since picking out the projects a state should budget and therefore defining scientific priorities is a very serious matter for the whole state and for each active and would-be scientist in it, I hold that the methodology of approving submitted projects should open and transparent and moreover, one project period (5 years) in advance.

I asked for a clarification at the Ministry and the enclosed reply I received from it indicates that the decision of making a list of projects to be budgeted was left entirely to the Scientific board. I contacted the members of the Scientific Board and they provided me with no further information or clue on their proposed list apart from some indications that they put the main weight on Impact Factors and citations.

Since I would like to get a budget for the project eventually, I thought it would be viable to make the following comparative analysis with respect to available data on the project and the coworkers who would work on it, on the one hand, and the 72 projects accepted to be budgeted, on the other. Its aim is to inform both our future evaluators and our colleagues in the World why our first attempt to get a budget failed. I believe this would speed up a recovery from damage the decision made by the Scientific Board inflicted on the research started for the project.
The details of the decision are as follows.
The Scientific Board "fully" accepts the referees' evaluation of the following project parts:
- Hypothesis and aims of the proposed research: 4.2 scores of 5 possible ones
- Plan and methodology of research: 4.13
- Feasibility of the project with the help of the existing equipment and available premises: 4.90;
- Evaluation of the budget the project asks for: 4.5.
The Scientific Board "fully" rejects the referees' evaluation of the following project parts:
- Competence of the head of the project: the scores of the referees - 5.00 and 4.66 - the Scientific Board "corrects" with a "factor of scientific productivity and impact of the head within the previous project period (2002-06)" to 2;
- Competence of the coworkers of the project: the scores of the referees - 2.5 and 4.1, the Scientific Board "corrects" with a "factor of scientific productivity and impact of the team within the previous project period (2002-06)" to 2.
The Scientific Board fully rejects advices of both referees to approve the budget for the project and for this it offers the following clarification:
- The average scores of the competence of the head and the coworkers of the project has after correction with a factor of international impact in the previous project period fallen below the passing level.
Besides, the Scientific Board comes to an unbelievable idea to utter the following "recommendation:" "M. Pavicic and S. Bosanac could concentrate themselves to the work on two other projects they take part in: M. Pavicic on the project No. 208 by M. Stipcevic and S. Bosanac on the project No. 1129 by N. Doslic."

The Scientific Board greatly oversteps its authorization when advising Pavicic and Bosanac what to engage themselves in. And just for the reader's information, the present project scarcely overlaps with the other two mentioned projects. Some results of one quarter of the project and only in the 4th and 5th year of research should be experimentally checked within the project 208 (if it obtains the equipment). There is no overlapping with the project 1129.

Here the reader might wonder how come the Scientific Board rejected such a project with such co-workers in spite of the positive evaluation of the referees and their recommendation to the Scientific Board to accept the project. The project boasts the creative excellence and would stimulate the scientific community in the World and Croatia. Yet, the Scientific Board writes about insufficient "international response and production in the previous project period (2002-06)."

The Scientific Board has not defined what the "international response and production" should be. They disregarded the "international response and production" the referees point out, so, they obviously mean something else. With reference to e-mails I received from two members of the Scientific Board I can guess that the "international response and production" boils to citations in Current Contents journals, to Impact Factors and to the number of Current Contents papers. We therefore introduce the following definitions of these "factors" in accordance to international bibliometric standards.
CC is a normed number of Current Contents papers per co-author in the following sense: if an author takes part in writing one paper together with 49 co-authors, two papers with 6 co-authors each and one paper with one co-author, then his/her CC=1/50+2/7+1/2=0.81.
CC measures the productivity of each co-author.

IF is a normed Impact Factor, i.e., an average "impact" per each co-author in the following sense: if an author takes part in writing one paper in a Current Contents journal with Impact Factor 7.489 together with 49 co-authors, two papers with Impact Factor 2.9976 with 6 co-authors each and one paper with Impact Factor 0.632 with one co-author, then his/her IF=7.489/50+2x2.997/7+0.632/2=1.32.
IF measures the "impact" of each co-author.

Ratio IF/CC is characteristic of the subfield an author works in but not for the "popularity" of the author him/herself and/or his results. For this, an indication offers the following citation factor.

CIT is a normed number of citations in Current Contents papers without self-citation and without cross self-citation per each co-author in the following sense: if a an author takes part in writing
- one paper together with 49 co-authors and this paper gets cited 80 times whereof 17 citations are made by co-authors of this paper,
- two papers each with 6 different co-authors and the first paper gets cited only once by one of the co-authors of the second paper and the second paper gets cited only once by one of the co-authors of the first paper and
- one paper with one co-author which gets cited twice by authors who never appear as co-authors of the author,
then CIT=63/50+0/7+0/7+2/2=2.26.
CIT measures the "popularity" of an author and/or his/her results with scientists in the same field around the Globe.

As we can see, the head and the co-workers and all together are better then at least 18 and at most 51 heads of the accepted projects. We have also tried various other combinations and definitions of Impact Factors, Current Contents and citations but could not win the last (73rd) place.

Of course, such bibliometric rank lists are always inherently inconsistent. On the one hand, for interdisciplinary projects Current Contents are not a priori significant. For instance, for mathematics and information science are not particularly important (McKay and Merlet have got the majority of papers and citations outside of Current Contents journals) and for civil engineering they are barely important (Fresl). On the other hand, inside physics itself Impact Factors and the number of Current Contents papers per year for mathematical and theoretical physics (Pavicic, Bosanac, Megill) are typically much lower and citations slower then, e.g., for astrophysics, nuclear physics, and particle physics.

To rely exclusively on such mechanical bibliometric comparison while making scientific priorities for a state and reject complex and individual analysis of referees as well as available data on the quality and originality of previous and current scientific work of the heads of proposed projects and their co-workers is a very worrying and disturbing idea. Besides, this is in sharp contradiction with the "importance of the referees for evaluating the projects" the Ministry repeatedly empahsized in the media.

However, there are even more worrying side of the present "case." Specifically, it is not only that the Scientific Board has not taken the evaluations and recommendations of the referees into account contrary to the emphasis of the Ministry and it is not only that it has not made its criteria public and that it has introduced them at its own will and without a consensus of the scientific community, but also that the Scientific Board apparently (as follows from the above comparison and the jumble CC, IF, and CIT make in the "72 ranking") has not applied unique criteria to all project proposals and this is even more worrying and disturbing.

The Race for Quality

The Cover Story of Jan 2007 Number of Humboldt Kosmos, the official journal of the Alexander von Humboldt Foundation is The Race for Quality. It is so appropriate for the above story that I thought it would be viable to copy the editorial introduction here. In particular because it speaks about "the consequences of rampant evaluation fever" and warns that "to an ever increasing degree, quality reviews are turning into a race for funding and reputation."

In the country of the blind, the one-eyed man is a king. This also seems to apply to the methods commonly used to measure academic quality. Reviewers are commissioned, publications and citations are counted, and journals are checked to see if papers have been published in them and if so, what the volume of readership is like. Of course, reviewers may be wrong occasionally. And there are things that simply can't be expressed by indexes which, moreover, are not immune to manipulation. But alternative methods of measuring research are not at hand. So one has to make the best of methods available, for instance by tailoring them to different disciplines and doing justice to individual cases. Chemist Roland Fischer gives an account of how this is done in the Humboldt Foundation's Selection Committee as an example.

The consequences of rampant evaluation fever, "evaluations," as the Science Council's Secretary General Wedig von Heyden calls it in this issue, are just as interesting as the proper methods. To an ever increasing degree, quality reviews are turning into a race for funding and reputation. While sciences and the humanities certainly aren't a hundred-metre race, the Olympic motto of "faster, higher, further" seems to count more and more in research. Here, good research and teaching need not necessarily be the winners. Unlike world records in sport, the desire to measure the achievements even of a research's entire lifetime and possibly express them in a single figure, as is the case with the Hirsch Index, tends to produce bizarre results.

Quality just happens to be a question of individuality and originality, as the topic of the leading articles shows. For instance, it is worthwhile for academics to try out swimming against the tide when choosing their research topics. And those taking a break from the hunt for quantifiable research achievements in order to allow for some unhurried academic activity may find that apparent idleness and patient trial and error, whether in the laboratory or at the desk, can sometimes yield the best ideas.

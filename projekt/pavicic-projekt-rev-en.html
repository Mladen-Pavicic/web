3.8 Summary: 

In the field of quantum computation the following research will be carried out:

(1)   Quantum algorithms

(2)   Theoretical description of quantum algebras

(3)   Quantum error correction

(4)   Theoretical design of quantum hardware for quantum computer.

 

The state of the art and our assumptions for (1)-(4) are

(1)   Would-be quantum computers assume algorithms of polynomial complexity. All known algorithms are based on the Fourier transform and reduce to finding eigenvalues of unitary operators. We will therefore concentrate on algorithms for computing genuine quantum problems.

(2)   Such algorithms require either (a) a discrete finite dimensional Hilbert space description of quantum systems for a discrete quantum computer or (b) a quasi-classical description for a continuous quantum computer. We shall work on deriving quantum algebras and Hilbert lattice equations for a possible direct implementation of a description of atomic and molecular states in a (discrete) quantum computer as well as on implementation of a probabilistic quasi-classical description of particle-field interaction in a continuous quantum computer.

(3)   The speed of a quantum computer depends on the efficiency of the quantum error-correction codes that can correct the states faster than they can decohere. We will work on obtaining algorithms for exhaustive generation of error-correction codes of arbitrary complexity.

(4)   Quantum hardware lately often relies on (a) atom-photon coupling and (b) manipulation of atom states. Our aim is to design quantum gates by means of (a) and (b) and use them as virtual test benches for outputs of (1)-(3) above.

Applications of the results obtained in the project will be in

(a) future development of quantum computation and quantum communication

(b) classical theory of computation

(c) quantum and classical cryptography

(d) theories of algebras and Hilbert lattices

(e) theory of atom-photon and atom-atom interactions

(f) discrete quantum theory

(g) methods of solving nonlinear equations

(h) graph theory

(i)  discretization methods in civil engineering

Methods of verifying (1)-(3 ) will heavily rely on classical parallel computing using cluster and grid technology available through the present programme and on visualization of physical and numerical process we cannot access directly because they are too small or too numerous. This visualization and the inherent parallelism of quantum computation give this project its name

8.3 Note : 
Our project involves complex computer algebras and demanding graphics programs and assumes cluster and Grid support. Program (which the present project is a part of) assumes including of the project into EGEE (Enabling Grids for E-sciencE – www.eu-egee.org). A way the project can achieve this is to become a node of EGEE. EGEE condition for that is that the project owns a server with at least 3 dual Xeon processors and other appropriate hardware characteristics taken into account when our server supplier formed the price given in 8.2.1. Thereupon the system administrators from the Institute Ruđer Bošković would connect the server to the SRCE (University Computing Centre) and project would be given access to EU Grid. This would speed up getting our results for several orders of magnitude with respect to the present conditions. We stress that the cluster in 8.1.2 cannot be used for the purpose, because it consists of a "pile" of student PCs of various configurations and speeds that are included in the cluster during the night while during the day students use them under MS Windows. We also stress that the server 8.1.1 cannot be included in an EGEE together with 2 other servers for technical reasons. Besides, it is 100% burdened with preparing input data for clusters. Graphics workstation is important for a visualization of our algorithms as we explained in the project. Visualization is also a backbone of the program our project is a part of .
